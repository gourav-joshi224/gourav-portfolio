## Why this comparison matters

After shipping three production systems at Unthinkable Solutions using both
Express and NestJS, I have some strong opinions about where each one helps and
where each one starts to hurt.

Express gives you raw flexibility. NestJS gives you structure. That difference
does not matter much in week one. It matters a lot once several engineers are
touching the same codebase, shipping features in parallel, and trying to keep
the system understandable six months later.

## What Express does well

Express is dead simple. You can have a working API in minutes and the mental
model is small enough that any Node.js developer can contribute immediately.

That simplicity is still valuable. For prototypes, internal tools, and narrow
services, Express often keeps you moving faster because there is very little
framework ceremony between the idea and the implementation.

## Where NestJS wins at scale

The dependency injection system alone saved us weeks of refactoring. Teams can
split work into clear modules, test services in isolation, and keep routing,
validation, business logic, and infrastructure concerns from bleeding into each
other.

The opinionated architecture also helps during onboarding. New developers can
usually predict where a controller, provider, DTO, or guard should live before
they even open the project.

## The tradeoff

NestJS is not free. You accept more abstraction, more conventions, and more
framework surface area. If the team does not need that structure yet, the
overhead can feel unnecessary.

My rule is simple: if the project is expected to stay small, Express is usually
enough. If the project needs to survive team growth, multiple domains, and long
term maintenance, NestJS starts paying for itself quickly.
